Wow, I can't believe it's been almost a year since my last blog post. The main reason is that high school has been keeping me busy (okay, that's just an excuse :D).

# Looking Back at This Year

Over the past year, I went through the high school entrance exam and COVID. Society went through the full reopening and the sudden emergence of ChatGPT. Even though there's so much worth talking about, I'll start with the little everyday things first.

## Middle School Days

I already covered the middle school part in my [previous article](https://blog.pi-dal.com/posts/Summary-Of-Junior-High-School-Life), so I'll just mention a few key moments:

1. Failed the entrance exam for the prestigious Shenzhen Nanshan Class in May (Napoleon triangles and chemical bonds really destroyed me, especially since the exam was announced the day before)
2. Had a week-long bout of high blood pressure in May
3. Took what might be the easiest high school entrance exam in the past decade in June
4. During my July trip, the education bureau changed their minds and released scores early, causing me to miss graduation

That's roughly my middle school experience. The exam results were average (at least in my opinion), but at least I didn't completely bomb it. I ended up going to CS — the same school I had once rejected.

## High School Life

### Summer Camp

My high school journey started in August with the competitive program selection camp. After an argument with my dad on July 31, I spitefully chose the physics track (originally I was going for informatics). I thought it'd be just a dozen people competing for a week — turns out it was 90+ people fighting for a handful of spots. I told my mom I probably wouldn't make it (mostly because I wasn't ready to give up my summer break). But then CS served a few days of amazing beef that won me over, and I decided I wanted to stay. Ended up placing third in the exam, and ultimately secured second place in the physics competitive program.

One fun thing — the summer camp organized a barbecue to boost our morale.

### School Life

The best part about being in the competitive program is that we **almost** didn't have to do military training. We trained just a little each day to get a feel for it — it was fun, and I even won first prize.

School started and everything fell into a routine. I quickly got to know my classmates. Teacher Sun was very encouraging. Everything seemed harmonious, and I was ready to make my mark. Then the first monthly exam hit me like a truck — I didn't even crack the top 100. That was a heavy blow, but it taught me that there's always someone better. I recovered a bit in the midterms (top 50), but the constant ups and downs have shown me how different high school academics are.

About the competitions — I chose physics. The content is fascinating and I genuinely enjoy it, so I've been putting in a lot of time practicing. Results haven't been great (otherwise I wouldn't be venting here).

CS competitive program activities: we can't join clubs or student council (not that we'd have the time anyway). But we still had street cleaning, sports day, and spring outings. I even participated in the shot put event and had my third ever bubble tea.

# On Learning

OK, after all that, let's talk about studying — the main source of stress in my high school life. Some people call me "Ye Shen," but I know I'm still a newbie. So far in freshman year, I've gone through high school physics, mechanics, and electromagnetism, almost finished advanced math, and memorized over a hundred pages of vocabulary. But I still have shortcomings:

1. I solve problems too fast without thinking deeply
2. I get nervous and hypoglycemic before exams
3. I'm too eager to prove myself
4. I lack perseverance in deep thinking

My plans to fix these:

1. Practice regularly to get used to exam conditions
2. Relearn how to take exams and manage time
3. Build a solid math foundation, then learn physics theory on top

I also plan to explore resources on weekends and use the Feynman technique for learning output.

# Philosophy

Finally, the topic I most want to discuss. High school has given me less time to think, but I've still had some unusual ideas (maybe Nietzsche's influence from middle school runs deep).

## On Ambition

ChatGPT's emergence has made many people anxious, but as a pessimist, I take it in stride. Technology will inevitably replace mindless labor. Can AI replace humans? I don't think so. AI can assist with repetitive tasks and to some extent "think," but I believe it can never achieve true self-awareness. ChatGPT hasn't passed the Turing Test. Neural networks, while resembling the human brain, can't be considered independent thinking. AI will be a helper, not a replacement for humanity's essential qualities.

I'm an idealist. I believe people should have their own judgment, not blindly follow trends. Be the one who sets the rhythm, not the one who follows it. Even if the world doesn't agree with you, even if your vision seems impossible, never lose your rationality and humanity. Too many people just go with the flow, letting the currents of the era carry them without independent thought.

## On Interpersonal Relations

This is my middle way. I particularly appreciate:

> When confronting someone, first be firm. If you find yourself outmatched, be flexible and adapt.

Some might call this weakness. I disagree — as long as the problem is solved, whether the other person agrees doesn't matter. Walk your own path.

Treat others sincerely, never backstab. Leave the benefits to others rather than taking them all for yourself. You might often get the short end, but a clear conscience is the best outcome.

## On Emotions

I'm a rather pessimistic person — Nietzschean pessimism.

I believe life is like enduring a trial. Perhaps death is the ultimate release. But we live not just for ourselves — we live for those who love us: parents, friends. We can't die for their sake.

This leads to my view on relationships:

1. Treat people sincerely, don't belittle or mock
2. Don't intentionally hurt others
3. Don't get married (more people caring about you means more people to grieve when you go, or vice versa)

Some might find my views twisted, but this is genuinely how I feel. Maybe one day I'll break out of this mindset.
