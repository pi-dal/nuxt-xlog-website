Today is June 28, 2022 — the day my high school entrance exam ended, and the day my junior high school life came to a close.

When primary school ended, I faced a choice: attend my current school's middle school division, or go to the best private middle school in the district. A good problem to have, but it took me a long time to decide. In the end, following my family's insistence, I chose to stay at my current school — one I knew all too well (hereafter referred to as "RS").

# Academic Life

## In Class

Entering RS's middle school division, I thought I was the main character (being in Class A, after all — meals were on the house). But once I got into class, I was shocked. I was student number 17, and numbers 16 and 18 were both top students from elementary school. The pressure was immense. When I introduced myself, all I could say was "the pressure is crushing." The first exam confirmed my fears — I only placed in the second tier. But I still had drive back then, and by midterms I ranked first in the entire grade — little did I know that would be the only time I'd ever achieve that. After that came the most painful lesson of my junior high career: I went all-in on the last math problem and lost everything, scoring my worst rank ever — 60-something. After that, I became much more cautious and stabilized in the top 10.

In the second semester of seventh grade, COVID hit and we switched to online classes. That was probably the most enjoyable period for me (I could slack off), and I used that time to learn a ton about computer technology. My grades remained relatively stable, hovering in the top 10.

By eighth grade, I became student number 1. That year, I achieved several top-3 placements, but only scored second-tier in the midterm exam — my second worst result! Academically, the year was unremarkable; the real focus was on extracurricular learning.

Entering ninth grade, the tension was immediate. Teachers constantly reminded us of the countdown to the high school entrance exam, and competition among classmates intensified. Fortunately, I performed steadily and managed to stay in the top tier.

Then came the much-anticipated exam. This year's test was surprisingly easy — many students felt their three years of study had been wasted. Ironically, this wasn't good for students at our school, especially someone like me who relied on difficult questions to pull ahead. And so, after three uneventful days of testing, my junior high school life came to an abrupt end.

## Extracurricular

Honestly, I found my extracurricular learning more interesting than classroom studies.

Over these three years, I was probably the student who won the most awards at RS. Beyond the regular exam certificates, I won prizes in district-level geography, math, English, and physics competitions (missing first place in English county-wide by half a point). I also earned awards in municipal and provincial computer science competitions (I built an OA system during online classes when I was bored, which ended up winning second prize at the provincial level).

I was also one of the most "titled" students at RS — District Outstanding Class Leader, Three Good Student, Municipal Outstanding League Member, Provincial Southern Guangdong Youth nominee...

These were probably the few things over three years that I could consider genuine achievements (or vanity).

# Rants

## Studying

To be honest, I wasn't really a grindset student (though some mistakenly thought I was). Before ninth grade, I never did practice problems beyond homework. But everything changed during the summer before ninth grade, when two neighbors "convinced" me to join the practice problem army. Once ninth grade started, due to the previous year's difficult math exam, I began grinding through countless problems — geometry puzzles, optimization problems, hidden circles (which I still don't fully understand). Many weeknights I studied past midnight, averaging less than 5.5 hours of sleep (one week I barely got 30 hours total). Yet nobody could have predicted this year's exam would be so easy.

## Health

The intense workload took a toll on my health. In the second semester of ninth grade, the stress caused hypoglycemia and hypertension — my blood pressure spiked to 150+/100+, and my heart rate fluctuated wildly, with a gap of nearly 150 bpm between highs and lows.

## Social Life

As much as I hate to admit it, I'm really not good at socializing. By ninth grade, almost the entire class had turned against me. I think the reasons are:

1. They thought I was clueless (though sometimes I was just pretending)
2. They thought I was humble-bragging (everyone has their own perspective)
3. They thought my intensity affected them (I wasn't intense — they were)
4. ...

Still, I was fortunate to have a group of supportive friends — from the "mur cat group" in seventh grade to my buddy Kai in ninth grade.

Overall, I'd say my social life was a complete failure.
