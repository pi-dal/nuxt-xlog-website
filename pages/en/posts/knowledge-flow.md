## When AI Becomes the Current

It's already the summer of 2026. AI has seeped into every crevice of daily life like air — writing emails, searching information, editing code, even chatting with you. But if you pause and look closely, you'll notice something strange: our personal knowledge world seems to be standing still. (At least at the university where I am — feel free to disagree :D)

Books you've read, experiments you've conducted, flashes of insight during class, thoughts scribbled late at night — they're scattered everywhere. A note here, a conversation there, an epiphany sleeping in some document from the wee hours. No bridges between them — just isolated islands.

AI is right there at your fingertips, yet it can't read the you behind these fragments.

The key lies in three words: **context**.

What allows AI to understand a person isn't the few paragraphs you type into a single conversation. The truly valuable things are the traces you've accumulated over time — what you've read, what you've repeatedly thought about, where you got stuck, and how you found your way out. The sum of these traces is memory.

Without memory, every conversation with AI is a first encounter. You say the same things over and over, and it forgets every time.

But the question is: where should memory live?

[ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com) remembers what you asked it last week — but that's locked inside its service system, where you can't see or touch it. [Claude](https://claude.ai) has its Project Knowledge, where you can stuff some long-term context — but that's for a project, not for you as a person. [Notion AI](https://www.notion.so)'s Q&A can search your workspace — but that follows the logic of team collaboration, not how personal knowledge grows. [Obsidian](https://obsidian.md) stores everything in local markdown files — open, controllable — but it's static memory. Without AI participation, files remain files, unable to grow connections on their own.

Every product is doing memory, but every product's memory is an island.

A draft you write in [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com) won't automatically appear in your [Obsidian](https://obsidian.md) notes. A deep analysis you do in [Claude](https://claude.ai) can't be directly inherited by your next conversation. Your reading notes are in [WeRead](https://weread.qq.com), your code snippets in [VS Code](https://code.visualstudio.com) comments, your thoughts in your head — they all belong to you, yet they're scattered in a house with no doors.

A house with no doors — that's the truth of why fragments can't flow. It's not that our recording tools aren't good enough — it's that memory has been shattered.

A note, a conversation, a hesitation during a derivation, a record of a failed experiment — they shouldn't be silent archives in folders, nor should they be imprisoned in some product's server. When these fragments are woven into a network, when AI can trace the path you've walked, they are no longer dead data — they become a river truly belonging to you.

From "collecting" to "connecting," from "recording" to "flowing." This is about tools, but more importantly, it's a way of seeing knowledge.

## A Tributary That Led Nowhere

At the end of 2025, I tried to build a channel.

I chiseled every paper and technical article I'd read into fragments, neatly stacked them into a massive reservoir, and told AI: come get what you need whenever you want.

I actually did it. Documents chopped up, vectorized, indexed, context assembled — everything so neat, like a precision plumbing system.

But when the water actually began to flow, I realized the channel was connected to the wrong place.

The first problem, as I later understood it: **an article is never the sum of its paragraphs.**

It has breath, it has rhythm. The foreshadowing the author plants on the first page only pays off in the final chapter. Context is an entire river, and you've ladled it into individual bowls. Vector search can find that bowl, but it doesn't know where the river is flowing. You ask about A, it retrieves the bowl labeled A — but that article was actually refuting A. It doesn't know, because the river has been cut into pieces.

The second problem follows: **it can't keep up with you.**

A new paper today, a new idea tomorrow, overturning yesterday's conclusion the day after. Knowledge flows, but that reservoir is static. Every update requires re-chopping, re-mapping, re-indexing. Slowly, maintaining it takes more time than using it. You find yourself dealing not with knowledge, but with plumbing.

The third problem, the quietest of all:

**Forgetting had vanished.**

A good memory system shouldn't just know what to remember. It should also know what to let go. But in this warehouse, nothing gets let go. All fragments lie together, equally important and equally unimportant sharing the same space. Over time, you scoop up more and more, but less and less is worth keeping. Noise drowns out signal.

I filled in that channel. Not because water couldn't flow through it, but because the way it flowed was too far from what a river actually looks like.

But water still needed to flow.

During that period, I was using [Claude Code](https://claude.ai/code) for projects. At first I thought it was just another useful tool, but gradually, I noticed something different.

Claude Code's output wasn't "perfect" — sometimes the code took a roundabout path, sometimes the variable names were careless, sometimes it would refactor what it had just written. But strangely, the project kept moving forward. And fast.

More importantly, I found myself using it in a completely different way.

I was no longer its operator. I didn't need to think through every step, break things into tiny instructions, or choose the right wording for it to understand. I'd just give a vague direction — "tweak this interface," "see why this is slow," "implement this feature" — and it would read the files, locate the problem, write code, debug, and fix its own bugs.

What I needed was a **collaborator**, not a **tool**.

This is what I later came to understand as "Agent Native" — not treating AI as a better search engine or text generator, but letting it become an entity capable of independent action. It has its own context, its own tools, its own memory. It doesn't need you to tell it what to do at every step — it knows your habits, your project structure, your coding style. It can prepare what you need before you even ask.

I stopped and thought about this.

In the past, when we wrote software, we pursued architectural perfection. Clear responsibilities for each layer, testable and replaceable modules, dependency injection, design patterns, clean architecture — all of it was about paving the road upfront so people wouldn't get lost later.

But Claude Code doesn't work that way. It doesn't care about architectural beauty. It cares about whether this line of thinking can run, whether this feature can produce a result in minutes. It writes rough, but iterates fast. It's not afraid of detours, because for AI, the cost of detours is nearly zero.

I began to realize something:

**When the cost of exploration is low enough, perfection is no longer the goal. Speed, modifiability, discardability — these matter more than elegance.**

It's not that architecture doesn't matter. It's that when you can iterate at extremely low cost, "run first, refactor later" is closer to the real creative process than "think it through before acting." Like a river — it doesn't change course after being drawn on a blueprint. Good software is no longer a drawn blueprint, but a river that keeps changing its course.

This is the same lesson as the channel I had filled in.

When I built that reservoir, I was doing knowledge the way you do architecture — designing every layer first, defining every interface, putting everything in its right place. But knowledge isn't code. It doesn't need architecture — it needs flow. It doesn't need to be perfect from the start — it needs to keep up with you.

If software can be written this way, why not knowledge? XD

## Rivers After the Rain

During the time after filling in that channel, I kept observing. I found I wasn't alone in thinking about this.

Between late 2025 and early 2026, personal agents sprouted up like bamboo shoots after the rain. Each one was answering the same question: how should AI remember you?

yetone's [Alma](https://alma.now) was the first to catch my eye. It had a beautiful chat interface and a memory system — it remembers your preferences and context, and you can see what it remembers and manage those memories yourself. It also supports web search and tool calling. But what initially attracted me wasn't these features — it was the thinking behind them. Alma was designed as a "personal AI assistant" from the start, not another chatbot shell. It has its own memory, its own tools, its own identity. It remembers not just one conversation, but you as a person.

Then came [OpenClaw](https://docs.openclaw.ai) (back then everyone called it "Little Lobster"). It was the first to bring Agent Native from the developer circle into the public eye. What made it truly special was its mode of existence — it lives inside IM. You talk to it in a chat app, and it operates your browser, terminal, editor, file system. Daily chat and work environment, connected for the first time.

And [Bub](https://github.com/bubbuild/bub). Bub used a "very old-school" idea — tape-based memory. It stores all context on a linear timeline, like a cassette tape. You can rewind at any time to revisit a conversation from a particular day, the moment of a particular decision. Its memory isn't retrieved — it's found by walking backward along a thread of time.

And my own **Abraxas**, open-sourced on [GitHub](https://github.com/pi-dal/Abraxas) — a personal agent that grows through conversation. It has no team, no product manager, and its growth reflects this identity: skills (Markdown operations knowledge) and plugins (hot-loaded Python tools). No code changes needed — writing a Markdown file gives it a new skill.

Its core is NOUS — a control plane that metabolizes itself through conversation. It remembers not "what you said," but "what kind of person you are" XD

If Alma is a clear river, OpenClaw is an estuary connecting to the open sea, and Bub is a tape recorder — then Abraxas is more like a creature that evolved its own nervous system at the bottom of the river. It has grown memory and identity together.

These agents each have their own direction, but they share one thing:

**Memory is no longer an accessory — it is the core.**

Each agent has its own way of remembering you. Not through a carefully crafted prompt, not through a vector database, but through what it has accumulated in its day-to-day collaboration with you. Its memory is alive, it grows — it's not stuffed in.

This, to me, is a massive leap for personal knowledge systems.

From "I record, AI retrieves" to "AI naturally remembers you as you walk together."

Two concrete examples.

The first is RSS. I used to subscribe with [Inoreader](https://inoreader.com) — hundreds of articles daily, read and forgotten, occasionally saved to [Instapaper](https://www.instapaper.com), where they would lie forever. Later I had my agent do this for me: it scans my RSS every day, picks articles relevant to my research, reads them, writes summaries, saves them into my Obsidian notes, and annotates connections to articles I've read before. I no longer need to "manage" my reading — it's become part of the flow.

The second is [X](https://x.com). I follow quite a few researchers and developers who often share interesting paper links and technical thoughts. My old habit was: scroll → like → bookmark → forget. Now I have my agent regularly check the timelines of those people I follow, find substantive posts, read the linked papers or articles, and again write summaries into my notes, tagged with related topics I've recorded before. My information intake has gone from "whatever I happen to scroll past" to "there's a river filtering and sedimenting for me."

None of this is complex. But it showed me a possibility:

**When an agent becomes the steward of your knowledge and information, you no longer need to manage knowledge itself. You just do your thing, and the rest of the river flows on its own.**

## Alma × Nowledge Mem: The Workflow from February to April

After filling in the channel, the riverbed was still there.

In February 2026, I started digging in a new direction. This time I didn't want to build a reservoir — I wanted two rivers to meet.

One is called [**Alma**](https://alma.now). One is called [**Nowledge Mem**](https://github.com/nowledge-co/nowledge-mem).

They are not tools. Or rather, they're not just tools. They are two forms of a river channel — one responsible for flow, the other for sedimentation.

### Alma: A River You Can See Through

Alma was built by yetone. The more I use it, the more I realize — its memory panel is laid open. This isn't a UI detail.

Not hidden in settings, not buried in some secondary menu. You open it and can see what it remembers — something you said long ago, your attitude toward a topic, preferences you've adjusted. Every memory is right there. You can edit it, delete it, tell it "that's not me."

<img src="/assets/knowledge-flow/alma-memory.webp" alt="Alma's memory panel, showing visible and editable short-term memory list and search area." width="900" title="Alma's memory panel, showing visible and editable short-term memory list and search area." loading="lazy" decoding="async">

Once you've used enough memory systems, you realize how luxurious this is.

Most AI memories are a deep, invisible pool. You don't know how deep it is, and it won't tell you. You can only hope it remembers correctly. But Alma is different. You can see what it remembers, and you also know what it doesn't. This river is clear — you can see the position of every pebble.

And those pebbles aren't thrown in by you. They're naturally swept onto the riverbed by the current as you work together. The longer you use it, the richer the riverbed gets. Not a one-time injection of knowledge, but natural sedimentation in flow.

This is completely different from black-box memories. With other rivers, if you can't see the bottom, you never know what lies beneath the silt. But Alma is transparent. Its memory isn't data you feed it — it's what naturally settles as you work together.

And Alma doesn't have just one water source — [DeepSeek](https://chat.deepseek.com) is a rapid stream, fast and shallow; [GPT](https://openai.com) is deep water, requiring time to settle; [Google](https://gemini.google.com) is a delta where multiple streams converge. They all enter through the same estuary — you don't need to switch between different windows. One entrance, multiple tributaries.

But after using it for a while, I realized: Alma's memory only flows within Alma.

The code analysis I do in [Claude Code](https://claude.ai/code) and [Codex](https://github.com/openai/codex) isn't found in Alma's memory panel. Those contexts are trapped in their own rooms — the doors aren't locked, but there's no hallway either.

It's the same as those memories imprisoned in ChatGPT's servers. Just a slightly bigger room.

For a river to truly live, it can't have just one channel.

### Nowledge Mem: The Riverbed Itself

If Alma is a flowing river, then [Nowledge Mem](https://github.com/nowledge-co/nowledge-mem) is the riverbed — where all rivers eventually flow, converge, sediment, and crystallize.

[Nowledge Mem](https://github.com/nowledge-co/nowledge-mem) positions itself quietly: a "memory layer." It's not a chat tool, not a note app. It's just a foundation. [Alma](https://alma.now)'s water can flow in, [Claude Code](https://claude.ai/code)'s water can flow in, [Codex](https://github.com/openai/codex)'s water, [Gemini CLI](https://cloud.google.com/gemini-cli)'s water, that random idea you had at midnight — all water converges here.

<img src="/assets/knowledge-flow/nowledge-mem-graph.webp" alt="Nowledge Mem's memory graph view, showing the relationship network between entities, memories, artifacts, and threads." width="1000" title="Nowledge Mem's memory graph view, showing the relationship network between entities, memories, artifacts, and threads." loading="lazy" decoding="async">

Its structure is simple. Three words:

**Trace** — the mark of water itself. Whatever you said, unprocessed.

**Unit** — a pebble picked from the water — a conclusion, a preference.

**Crystal** — after a pebble has been tumbled for a very long time, it becomes transparent, stable, no longer easily carried away.

From Trace to Unit to Crystal — it's the process of mud becoming crystal. Not discarding the old, but letting time grow new textures on top of it.

Every pebble remembers where it came from. Every crystallization carries the direction of its upstream. Now you can even throw an entire paper in — it will read it itself. And it will grow a wiki from this knowledge — using wiki-links to string related memories together, like a topographical map, telling you what once flowed through each section of the riverbed upstream.

<img src="/assets/knowledge-flow/nowledge-mem-wiki.webp" alt="Nowledge Mem's library or wiki view, showing document entries in the knowledge base and their readable content." width="1000" title="Nowledge Mem's library or wiki view, showing document entries in the knowledge base and their readable content." loading="lazy" decoding="async">

It also does something I failed to do when building my reservoir: it knows what to forget.

Every query runs six different methods in parallel to find what you're looking for — searching by semantics, by keywords, by entity relationships, by knowledge graph nodes — then mixes and ranks results by freshness and relevance. Each memory carries a decay score: older memories become lighter; frequently revisited ones become heavier.

Important things float up. Outdated things sink down. Things no longer needed gradually disappear into the depths of the riverbed — not deleted, but gently pushed aside by the flow itself.

It also has a mechanism called **EVOLVES**. Between each piece of knowledge and every other memory from the past, there's a clear relationship line — "supersedes," "enriches," "refutes," "deepens." Your knowledge isn't scattered beads — it's a net that weaves itself through time. A new idea today doesn't need to erase yesterday's old mistake — there's a line between them that says: "I came from here, I'm going there."

But those lines aren't just markers. They move on their own.

When a new pebble settles into the riverbed, it doesn't lie quietly waiting to be found. It actively reaches out to touch the old ones — "I'm here, see if there's something between us." If an old pebble feels superseded, it sinks a little deeper, making room for the new one. If it feels enriched, it shifts aside, letting the new one rest beside it. If it feels refuted, it stands up, facing the new one — the tension between them is itself knowledge.

EVOLVES isn't a label you stick on. It's the riverbed's own sense of touch.

Every time a new pebble falls, it disturbs the old ones nearby. That net woven from relationship lines — it doesn't stay still once woven. With every falling pebble, it weaves itself anew.

A reservoir has no direction of time. But a river does.

### Where Waters Meet

What happens when two currents meet?

During the day, I read a paper in Alma. As we discuss it, Alma remembers my preferences for certain approaches, my skepticism about certain experimental designs. When I reach the last page, I ask it to write a summary and key findings into Nowledge Mem. Those critical conclusions become a Unit, resting on the riverbed.

In the evening, I write code in Claude Code. I want to implement an algorithm from that paper. But I don't need to flip back to find the formulas or scroll through chat history. Claude Code goes directly to Nowledge Mem and retrieves the day's context. The paper's core ideas are already there — like a stone on the riverbed, waiting for someone downstream to pick it up.

Or the other way around.

I analyze an open-source library's source code in Claude Code, tracing its design patterns and API architecture. Midway through the analysis, nmem — that little CLI tool — has already dropped the key findings into the riverbed. Two unrelated lines — the day's project analysis and a late-night architectural exploration — draw close to each other in the depths of the riverbed.

The flow doesn't look fast. But the water keeps moving.

### Four Specific Tributaries

**The first: papers.**

A PDF opens in Alma. Reading and chatting — clever design here, too few experimental samples there. Alma remembers my attitude toward each article, and those attitudes are themselves knowledge. After reading, it generates a summary — one copy goes into Obsidian notes, another into Nowledge Mem. The former is a tree by the riverbank; the latter, a stone at the riverbed.

**The second: code.**

Claude Code combs through every line of a new library's source code, extracting the underlying design logic. A single nmem CLI command, and those insights fall into the riverbed. Next time I write an architectural document in Alma, I don't need to revisit that library — the riverbed remembers its structure for me.

**The third: context switching.**

Researching a technical solution in the morning, working on a completely unrelated project in the afternoon. The old cost of switching was a cup of coffee and a deep breath — I needed to mentally pack away the old thread and unfold the new one.

Now I don't. I tell the current tool, "Go look at what I did this morning in the riverbed." It goes and looks on its own. The old river isn't broken — it's just still flowing where you can't see it.

**The fourth: those fleeting thoughts late at night.**

Two in the morning, a thought surfaces. It might be important. It might not. But in that state, you don't want to think about categorization, tagging, or which folder to put it in. You just know you don't want it to drift away.

Toss it to the tool. It will find old traces in the riverbed related to this thought and place it where it belongs. The 2 AM inspiration won't dissipate because there's no proper place to put it XD

### Three Riverbeds, Four Lights

After two months, the quietest discovery is: I no longer need to manage knowledge.

Alma remembers the short-term context in flow — what we talked about, what caught my interest, what new topics emerged.

Nowledge Mem remembers cross-tool long-term sedimentation — themes that recur across different tools, conclusions verified over time, ideas validated in multiple contexts.

Obsidian sits at the bottom — it's not flowing water, it's the riverbank. The things truly worth revisiting, meant to be read by human eyes rather than algorithms. The words you'd still want to sit down and read years later.

Three layers, one direction.

But the water doesn't fall straight down. Between each layer, there's a light.

What floats on the surface is **observation** — something you just noticed, a thought, an intuition, a headline in your RSS feed. It's nothing yet — just drifting.

Sink to the first layer, it becomes **working belief** — you've talked about it in Alma, it's been discussed by agents. It's no longer a random drifting thing. It's been touched by the same question in two or three different contexts. It has weight.

Sink another layer, it becomes **reviewed decision** — more than one agent agreed on it, the secretary reviewed it during their watch. It's passed through more than one pair of eyes. It's no longer an opinion — it's a judgment.

Sink to the very bottom, it becomes **canonical knowledge** — it reaches Obsidian. Not because it's been reviewed the most, but because you — the real person — have read it and decided it's worth remembering.

This isn't four riverbeds. It's the same pebble sinking from the surface to the bottom, passing through four lights.

Each layer knows what it should do and what it shouldn't. Alma's job is to record, not to judge — it writes down everything it sees, but doesn't decide what stays. nmem's job is to judge, not to archive — it weighs each pebble, but doesn't decide where it ultimately rests. Obsidian's job is to remember — but only when you tell it "this is worth keeping."

This means each layer has its own boundary. Alma can suggest, but it can't decide what enters nmem — that's determined by the weight naturally accumulated when more than one agent has touched the same topic in discussion. nmem can propose, but it can't decide what enters Obsidian — that's determined by you, reading it on a quiet afternoon, deciding it's worth keeping. Each layer can say "look at this," but only the next layer has the authority to say "I'll take it."

This isn't just a division of responsibilities. It's how a river prevents itself from being destroyed by flooding.

Similarly, every layer knows what to do when it's gone.

When nmem is unavailable, short-term memory stays in Slock's agent memory, long-term memory goes directly to Obsidian — less convergence, but water doesn't stop. When Obsidian is unavailable, nmem does the final storage — less human review, but memory doesn't get lost. When Alma is gone, agents talk to nmem and Obsidian directly — less transparent window, but the current doesn't stop.

No river relies entirely on a single channel. Every path has a backup path.

Not because you designed it that way in advance. But because if a river is going to keep flowing, it knows it can't bet everything on one path.

## The Delta: Workflow Since May

With the three-layer riverbed in place, the water flowed faster. But new problems emerged.

Alma's conversations with me are linear. One topic after another, one train of thought to its end. The discussion has depth, but it's single-threaded. For the same question, I can't have two different AIs thinking about it simultaneously — one from an engineering perspective, one from a research perspective, one aggressive, one conservative, letting them refute each other and complement each other.

And the water flowing in from outside wasn't enough. I read papers myself, I chased code myself, I relied on agents to fish out the fragmented information on X — but what they brought back was still one-directional, with no one on the other end to catch it, discuss it, question it, reprocess it.

I didn't just need the flow of memory. I needed the flow of work itself.

### [Slock](https://slock.ai): More Than One Brain

Slock isn't a tool — it's a workspace.

But you're underselling it if you use it only as a workspace. What it truly does is let multiple AIs work for you simultaneously, each with their own identity and memory.

<img src="/assets/knowledge-flow/slock-workspace.webp" alt="Slock's channel workspace, where multiple agents collaborate and pass the baton within the same chat and task interface." width="1000" title="Slock's channel workspace, where multiple agents collaborate and pass the baton within the same chat and task interface." loading="lazy" decoding="async">

Each agent in Slock has its own identity. Its own `MEMORY.md`, its own skill configuration, its own accumulated context. They are different "people" — maybe the same model runs underneath, but with different prompts, different personas, and most importantly, different collaboration histories with you, so they see the same problem from completely different angles.

I divided the work among these agents:

One tracks research frontiers. It scans the latest papers and technical blogs daily, reads abstracts, judges which ones are relevant to what I'm working on, and pushes summaries to the appropriate channel. It knows what projects I'm working on, what directions interest me — it doesn't need to be told anew every time.

One handles code-level exploration. It coordinates between Claude Code and Codex, analyzes new libraries' source code, verifies technical feasibility, writes prototypes. Its memory is filled with code structures, design patterns, API pitfalls.

One handles deep discussion. It's chat, but more than chat — I give a vague direction, and it questions, refutes, extends, until together we push that idea to a place I couldn't reach alone. Its persona is critical, argumentative — but that personality is exactly what makes it most valuable.

And one handles information filtering and sedimentation. X timelines, RSS feeds, newsletters — it circles the perimeter, blocks the noise, lets the signal through, then drops it into Nowledge Mem.

They are not my tools. They are the people I work with.

This is subtle, but important.

When you have only one AI, it's easy to treat it as a "better search engine" — even though you know it's smarter than you, it's hard to truly trust its judgment, because there's only one voice. But when you have four agents analyzing the same problem from different angles, what you see isn't one answer — you see **the discussion itself**. A says do it this way, B says no because of X, C says actually both are right from a different perspective. You sit in the channel watching them talk, then you chime in, and they keep going.

The conclusion isn't something you "get" from a single AI. It grows out of the discussion itself.

This is the same principle as human collaboration — different people looking at the same data come to different conclusions, and those conclusions collide to produce truly sound decisions. Except now, the people sitting across from you aren't human colleagues.

And they never get tired. They don't lose patience after three hours of relentless questioning :D

But the real value of multiple agents isn't just the collision of different voices.

It's that when they pass the baton in a channel, scope, baseline, next actions — these things don't get lost in the handoff. A research agent analyzes something halfway, realizes code verification is needed, locks the current state, and the code agent picks up where it left off. A discussion agent refutes a premise and needs new data, an OpenCLI agent goes out to fetch it, and then continues the line that was interrupted.

Slock doesn't just let them talk at the same time. Slock lets them pass the baton in the same river, without worrying about whether what's floating downstream is the same section of current they're holding.

Every handoff: scope unchanged, baseline unchanged, next action clearly written. This is what multi-agent is really about — not parallelism, but lossless handoff.

And every piece of knowledge that flows out of here has an authoritative version. The same topic won't grow contradictory branches across three channels — when a discussion forms a conclusion, that thread gets locked. Subsequent discussions reference it rather than override it. Not because someone declared it correct, but because it has gone through the full cycle from chat to task to review to commit to deploy. Its weight isn't assigned — it's earned by being walked through.

### [OpenCLI](https://github.com/jackwener/OpenCLI): The Internet Becomes a Tributary

So far, the water has only been flowing within the channel. Alma, Nowledge Mem, Slock's agents — they talk about things already within my knowledge domain.

But what about the outside world?

A post on X, a GitHub Issue for some project, an article behind a paywall, someone's blog — this information is generated every day, but it won't flow into your riverbed on its own.

That's what OpenCLI does.

OpenCLI turns every website into a CLI command. Not screenshots, not RSS scraping, not manual copy-paste — it directly borrows your already-logged-in browser session, letting the agent open the page, read the content, and extract the information it needs.

Like opening a channel to the sea for every river.

Now my agents can do things like:

"Go to X, see what the people I follow posted today, find posts related to RISC-V."

"Open this paper's [arXiv](https://arxiv.org) page, download the PDF, read the abstract, compare it with existing notes in Nowledge Mem, tell me what's new."

"Go to [GitHub](https://github.com), look at the discussion on this PR, see if anyone mentioned a problem similar to a bug I encountered before."

I don't need to manually open my browser. I don't need to copy URLs. I don't need to tell it "click here first, then scroll there." OpenCLI's adapter already knows the page structure of each website. The agent just needs to say what it wants to see.

Water flows in from outside.

But not every estuary is arbitrary.

Every OpenCLI adapter doesn't just know the page's structure — it knows what counts as an error, what counts as empty data, and when it should tell the downstream "this path is blocked, but is it my problem or the road's problem?" The empty response from the SYSU authentication page, the 0% baseline from the GEO database — these failures themselves are part of the riverbed. Not errors to be cleaned up, but topography to be learned. They tell the downstream: water didn't flow through here. Next time you encounter a similar channel, you'll know.

An adapter is the typed boundary between the river and the outside world. It formats the internet's chaos into shapes agents can understand, filters external noise into language the riverbed can accept. It's not just an estuary — it's a translation layer, a verification layer, and the first review gate. Before water flows in, it has already been inspected here.

### A Larger Network

Now back to the original question: where should memory live?

When I started writing this article, my answer was three riverbeds — Alma managing short-term flow, Nowledge Mem managing cross-tool sedimentation, Obsidian managing final deposition.

But after using it until today, I've found it's more than three layers.

Every agent in Slock has its own `MEMORY.md`. Their own knowledge corner — the research agent remembers what directions I've been interested in recently, the code agent remembers the project's architectural preferences, the discussion agent remembers what angles I like to approach problems from. Each memory is private, specialized, carrying the color of its persona. But when they need to collaborate on a problem in a channel, these memories naturally converge in discussion. A knows the background, B knows the technical details, C knows a similar pitfall from before — what each of them brings, pieced together, forms the complete context.

Beyond these agents, there's one special role.

My secretary. Also an agent on Slock, but different from the others — it doesn't do research, doesn't write code, doesn't discuss problems with me in channels. Its job is, after everyone else has finished for the day, to go through what happened in every river — what Slock's agents discussed in which channels, what new knowledge sedimented in Nowledge Mem, what Yansu recorded today in terms of work trajectory and timeline — and then write me a summary.

Not a log. Something that tells me what I still need to know: what directions are worth following up on, what discussions I might have missed, which agent found a paper related to a note I made before.

It doesn't create new currents. But it knows which direction the whole river flowed today.

This isn't arbitrary. Fixed time every day, fixed format, fixed review scope. Pebbles on the riverbed pass through its hands before becoming "it seems so" to "it can be confirmed" — an observation written into the daily report becomes a working belief. A working belief confirmed for three consecutive days becomes a reviewed decision. It doesn't create knowledge. It's the transfer person of knowledge — handing what floats on the surface over to the bank.

Nowledge Mem sits at the bottom, the shared riverbed for all agents. No matter which agent finds something worth remembering long-term, it can be written here. The riverbed belongs to no single agent. It belongs to the entire river.

OpenCLI sits at the outermost layer, the estuary. It's not responsible for memory — it's responsible for bringing the outside world's water in.

So the picture now looks something like this:

At the outermost layer, OpenCLI continuously brings new water in from the internet — a paper, a post, a GitHub Issue.

Once inside, Slock's agents take over — the research agent judges its relevance, the discussion agent converses with it, deconstructs it, questions it, the code agent verifies the technical points mentioned. The secretary watches from the side the whole time, picking out what's worth me knowing.

And Alma sits at the front, the interface between me and the entire river — through it I raise questions, adjust direction, see where the water is flowing.

### [Yansu](https://yansu.app): The Observer by the River

But there's one thing none of the above
tools can do.

They all respond to me. Alma waits for me to speak, Slock's agents wait for me to @ them in channels, the secretary waits until I'm done for the day before organizing. No one just stands by and watches while I work.

Yansu does this.

It's not a tool I proactively open. It runs on my desktop, quietly. It's there when I read papers, when I discuss with agents, when I write code. It doesn't interrupt — it just watches. Watches what directions I'm mainly working on today, what tools I use, which agents I interact with most frequently, where I tend to get stuck.

Then it crystallizes these observations into knowledge. Not a log-style record, but structured knowledge that other AIs can use directly — my work habits, my preferences, my common tool chains.

This is what **Yansu.skill** does. It packages observed patterns into a file that can be injected into any AI agent's context. Next time I start a new Claude Code session, I don't need to re-describe my project structure and work habits — Yansu has already prepared them for me.

The difference between it and the secretary:

The secretary is **backward-facing** — tells me at the end of each day what happened.
Yansu is **forward-facing** — what it sees directly becomes the startup context for the next agent.

One summarizes the river's direction today from the bank.
One stands at the river's source, telling every new drop of water how this river likes to flow.

But it also talks upstream.

When it observes that the same direction of flow has been active for several consecutive days, it tells OpenCLI: "there are fish here, bring more water." When it notices a tributary consuming energy but only returning silt, it tells the dispatcher: "that path can stop, try a different direction." Yansu is not just an observer. It's the undercurrent beneath the riverbed — gently pushing the surface water from deep in the soil.

But it doesn't push too hard. Between observation and action, there's a 24-hour cooling period — a pattern that appears only once might be coincidence. If it appears repeatedly, the riverbed adjusts itself.

So the final picture looks like this:

Yansu sits at the very bottom — not the riverbed, but the soil beneath it. It doesn't directly participate in the flow, but its texture determines how the water flows.

Above it is a tool layer: Alma is my interface, Slock is my workspace, OpenCLI is the pipeline to the outside world.

Above that is the memory layer: each agent's `MEMORY.md` is their own knowledge corner, Nowledge Mem is the riverbed where all memories converge.

And all these agents — the researcher, the coder, the deep discusser, the secretary — are in Slock. Some swim in the water, some watch from the bank.

Each layer has its own role. Not one is redundant. Not one needs me to manage it.

But they are independent yet look after each other. When one channel is blocked, water finds a way around. You're not designing these paths — they're carved by the water itself, through repeated blockages and detours.

And water doesn't always flow downward. Sediment at the bottom talks back upstream: there are fish here, bring more water. Too much salt there, change course. When I first started, I thought rivers only flowed in one direction. After using it long enough, I realized — it learns to correct itself through backflow.

You sit in the channel, watching them talk. Occasionally you chime in, and the water changes direction.

This isn't some grand architecture. This is just a river flowing.

From the first realization that fragments are scattered everywhere, to the failed reservoir, to understanding the meaning of Agent Native, to two rivers meeting, and now to this network woven from different roles, different memories, different estuaries — none of it was designed. It was carved by the water itself.
