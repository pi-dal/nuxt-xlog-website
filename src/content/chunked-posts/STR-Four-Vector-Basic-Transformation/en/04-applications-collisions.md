## Applications — Elastic and Inelastic Collisions

### Elastic Collisions

In elastic collisions, both four-momentum and rest mass are conserved. For two-particle collisions $A + B \to A + B$, we can write

$$P_A + P_B = P'_A + P'_B$$

Using the invariant $P_A \cdot P_B$, we can derive the scattering angle and energy transfer formulas. For example, the Mandelstam variables

$$s = (P_A + P_B)^2 = (P'_A + P'_B)^2$$
$$t = (P_A - P'_A)^2 = (P_B - P'_B)^2$$
$$u = (P_A - P'_B)^2 = (P_B - P'_A)^2$$

are Lorentz-invariant quantities that simplify cross-section calculations.

### Inelastic Collisions and Energy Loss

For inelastic collisions where particles coalesce, $A + B \to C$, momentum conservation gives

$$P_A + P_B = P_C$$

The mass of the resulting particle satisfies

$$M_C^2 c^2 = (E_A + E_B)^2 - (\mathbf{p}_A + \mathbf{p}_B)^2 c^2$$

This shows that $M_C$ is generally larger than $m_A + m_B$, with the extra mass coming from the kinetic energy converted to internal energy.

### Compton Scattering

Compton scattering $\gamma + e \to \gamma + e$ is a classic example where four-vector methods simplify calculations. The change in photon wavelength

$$\lambda' - \lambda = \frac{h}{m_e c}(1 - \cos\theta)$$

can be derived directly from four-momentum conservation without complex algebra.
